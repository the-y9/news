## International Relations
#### Headline: India successfully tests Solid Fuel Ducted Ramjet (SFDR) technology, enhancing air-to-air missile capabilities.
  - India, in collaboration with Russia, has successfully tested the Solid Fuel Ducted Ramjet (SFDR) technology.
  - This technology is a missile propulsion system that uses a ramjet engine, an air-breathing jet engine that compresses incoming air to create high pressure for combustion.
  - The SFDR technology will enhance the combat effectiveness and range of India's long-range air-to-air missiles.
  - Missiles equipped with SFDR technology fly at supersonic speeds, are highly maneuverable, and difficult to intercept as they obtain oxygen from the atmosphere.
  - This technological advancement places India among a select few countries possessing this capability.

#### Headline: NAMASTE Scheme aims to mechanize sanitation work and eliminate manual scavenging.
  - The National Action for Mechanized Sanitation Ecosystem (NAMASTE) Scheme, a joint initiative of the Ministry of Social Justice and Ministry of Housing and Urban Affairs, aims to mechanize sanitation work.
  - Launched in 2022, the scheme's primary objective is to replace manual scavenging with machine-based cleaning.
  - Profiling of sanitation workers in urban areas is being conducted to identify those engaged in waste picking and sanitation.
  - Data reveals that a significant majority (84%) of identified sanitation workers belong to Scheduled Caste, Scheduled Tribe, and Other Backward Classes communities.
  - Some states like Delhi and Goa show a higher proportion of sanitation workers from the General Category.
  - The scheme is implemented by the National Safai Karamchari Finance and Development Corporation and aims to provide health insurance, safety measures, and rehabilitation to sanitation workers.

#### Headline: Supreme Court's approach to death penalty cases highlights concerns over judicial adherence to guidelines.
  - The Supreme Court has not confirmed any death penalty sentences in the last three years, though lower courts continue to award them.
  - Lower courts are observed to frequently award the death penalty, often without strictly adhering to the "rarest of rare" doctrine established by the Supreme Court.
  - In 2023, lower courts sentenced 128 individuals to death, but the confirmation rate by High Courts and the Supreme Court remains very low.
  - The Supreme Court, in cases like Bachan Singh vs. State of Punjab (1980), has laid down guidelines for awarding the death penalty, including the "rarest of rare" principle, requiring brutal murders, depraved motives, and significant crimes.
  - Guidelines also stipulate a minimum 15-day gap between conviction and sentencing to allow for psychological preparation, which is often overlooked by subordinate courts.
  - The constitutionality of the death penalty has been upheld, but its application remains a subject of debate due to concerns about fairness, deterrence, and human rights.
