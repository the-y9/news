## Polity
#### Headline: Budget Presentation and Parliamentary Procedures
  - The Union Budget is presented by the **Ministry of Finance**, specifically the **Budget Division** within the **Department of Economic Affairs**.
  - A budget must be passed by the Lok Sabha; failure to do so can lead to the government losing confidence and the Prime Minister resigning.
  - The term "budget" is *not explicitly mentioned in the Indian Constitution*; instead, Article 112 refers to it as the **"Annual Financial Statement".**
  - The concept of presenting the budget in a briefcase originated with British Budget Chief William E. Gladstone in *1860*.
  - **R.K. Shanmukham** Chetty presented the first independent Indian budget in 1947.
  - Nirmala Sitharaman holds the record for the longest budget speech in terms of duration, while Manmohan Singh's longest speech was in terms of word count.
  - Historically, the budget was presented in the evening at 5 PM; this was changed to 11 AM by Yashwant Sinha.
  - Arun Jaitley shifted the budget presentation date to February 1st, and this has been followed since 2016.
  - Prior to 2017, the Railway Budget was presented separately; it has since been merged with the Union Budget.
  - Only three Prime Ministers (Jawaharlal Nehru, Indira Gandhi, and Rajiv Gandhi) have presented the budget.
  - Morarji Desai has presented the most budgets (10 times), with Nirmala Sitharaman closely following.
  - **Article 280** of the Constitution establishes the **Finance Commission** to distribute central tax revenue among states.
  - The 16th Finance Commission's recommendation for states to receive **41%** of divisible central taxes has been accepted.
  - Union Territories do not have a separate budget as they fall under the direct control of the Central Government.

## Economy
#### Headline: Union Budget 2026: Receipts, Expenditures, and Fiscal Health
  - The Union Budget for 2026-27 projects total receipts of ₹53.4 lakh crore, comprising ₹18.1 lakh crore from Capital Receipts and ₹35.3 lakh crore from Revenue Receipts.
  - Total expenditure for 2026-27 is estimated at ₹58.4 lakh crore, with ₹17 lakh crore allocated for Capital Expenditure and ₹41.3 lakh crore for Revenue Expenditure.
  - The government's **primary sources of revenue** include: 
    1. borrowings and liabilities (24%), 
    2. income tax (21%), 
    3. corporate tax (18%), 
    4. GST and other indirect taxes (15%), and 
    5. non-tax revenue (10%).
  - Government **expenditures** are primarily allocated to:
    1. state share of taxes (22%),
    2. interest payments (20%),
    3. central sector schemes (17%),
    4. and defense (11%).
  - The *fiscal deficit* for 2026-27 is projected to be approximately *₹5 lakh crore*, reflecting an expenditure > revenue.
  - The budget emphasizes increasing capital expenditure to boost infrastructure development and economic growth.
  - Revenue receipts are defined as income that does not create a liability or reduce government assets, including tax and non-tax revenue.
  - Capital receipts include borrowings, recovery of loans, and disinvestment, which either create a liability or reduce government assets.
  - Revenue expenditure includes day-to-day operational costs like salaries, pensions, and subsidies, which do not create assets.
  - Capital expenditure is considered growth-oriented as it involves spending on assets like infrastructure (roads, railways, buildings), which can generate future income.
  - The government aims to reduce the debt-to-GDP ratio to 50% (+/- 1%) by 2030. The current ratio for 2024-25/2025-26 is 56.1%.
  - The budget includes a ₹100 crore fund for MSME Growth Fund and the establishment of "Corporate Mitra" to support small businesses.
  - A "Trade Receivables e-Discounting System" (TReDS) platform will mandate transaction settlements for MSMEs to ensure timely payments.
  - The government plans to invest in the India Semiconductor Mission 2.0, biopharma research, and the production of rare earth permanent magnets.
  - Initiatives for the textile sector, electronic manufacturing, and MSMEs are planned, including customs duty exemptions on raw materials for manufacturing.
  - The budget allocates ₹7.85 lakh crore for defense in 2026, a 15% increase.
  - Education spending for 2026-27 is projected at ₹1.39 lakh crore, a slight increase from the previous year's ₹1.28 lakh crore.
  - The government is focusing on urbanization, with plans for seven high-speed rail corridors and development in Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities.
  - The budget proposes developing temple towns and improving the ease of doing business.
  - Government transfers to states and Union Territories are projected at ₹26.2 lakh crore for 2026-27.
  - Focus areas for development include coconut and cashew cultivation, and the wood sector.
